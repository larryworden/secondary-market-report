Five Things Secondary Market Operators Need to Know
1
The memory crisis is structural, not cyclical.
Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron have permanently reallocated DRAM and NAND capacity to AI datacenters. New fab capacity won't reach volume until 2027. The sub-$100 smartphone segment — 171M devices — is, in IDC's words, permanently uneconomical. There is no return to business as usual.
2
Brand selection has never mattered more for resale value.
Apple led the global primary market for the first time in Q1 2026 with 21% share and +5% YoY growth — the only top brand growing. iPhones retain 60–70% of value after two years. Xiaomi fell -19% YoY, the sharpest decline among the top five. The secondary market mirrors whoever wins new.
3
The Middle East is a compound crisis in real time.
IDC projects Middle East & Africa new smartphone shipments down -20.6% in 2026 — the steepest regional decline globally. The Strait of Hormuz closure has disrupted Jebel Ali, suspended Emirates SkyCargo, and added 2–4 weeks to electronics logistics. The memory shortage and geopolitical shock are compounding simultaneously in one of the world's highest-growth secondary markets.
4
Supply is the defining constraint — and the biggest opportunity.
An estimated 150 million high-value smartphones sit unused in US homes. The organized take-back rate from primary upgrades is just 17%. ecoATM collected 7.5M devices across 7,000 US kiosks in 2025. Operators who unlock latent supply — through better trade-in programs, kiosk access, and carrier partnerships — will own this cycle.
5
Capital is professionalizing the market at speed.
Over $4.3 billion flowed into secondary market infrastructure in 2024. Back Market raised $510M. Refurbed acquired Swappie. Samsung, Google, and Apple all launched new certified trade-in programs in 2025. The competitive landscape is professionalizing faster than many operators anticipated.
